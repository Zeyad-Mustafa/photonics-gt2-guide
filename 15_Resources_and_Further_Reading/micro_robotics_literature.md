# Micro-Robotics Literature

## Overview

This file covers the key published work on microrobots fabricated by two-photon polymerization, including magnetic swimmers, soft actuators, cargo carriers, and shape-morphing devices.

---

## Foundational Microrobotics Physics

Purcell, E. M.
Life at low Reynolds number.
American Journal of Physics, 45(1), 3-11, 1977.
DOI: 10.1119/1.10903

This is the foundational paper for microrobotics physics. Purcell's lecture explains why locomotion at low Reynolds number is fundamentally different from macroscale swimming, introduces the scallop theorem (reciprocal motion produces no net displacement), and identifies helical rotation as the simplest non-reciprocal swimming strategy. Required reading before designing any microswimmer. The paper is entirely accessible without advanced mathematics and is one of the most enjoyable reads in all of physics.

---

Lauga, E. and Powers, T. R.
The hydrodynamics of swimming microorganisms.
Reports on Progress in Physics, 72(9), 096601, 2009.
DOI: 10.1088/0034-4885/72/9/096601

Comprehensive review of the hydrodynamics of microorganism locomotion, covering resistive force theory, helical propulsion, flagellar mechanics, and near-wall swimming. This is the standard theoretical reference for microswimmer design and is required reading for anyone doing quantitative design of helical microswimmers.

## Magnetic Helical Microswimmers

Zhang, L., Abbott, J. J., Dong, L., Kratochvil, B. E., Bell, D., and Nelson, B. J.
Artificial bacterial flagella: Fabrication and magnetic control.
Applied Physics Letters, 94(6), 064107, 2009.
DOI: 10.1063/1.3079655

One of the early demonstrations of artificial bacterial flagella — helical microswimmers fabricated by a self-scrolling nanofilm process and actuated by rotating magnetic fields. Not 2PP-fabricated, but defines the design concept and characterization methodology that GT2-fabricated helical swimmers follow.

---

Tottori, S., Zhang, L., Qiu, F., Krawczyk, K. K., Franco-Obregon, A., and Nelson, B. J.
Magnetic helical micromachines: Fabrication, controlled swimming, and cargo transport.
Advanced Materials, 24(6), 811-816, 2012.
DOI: 10.1002/adma.201103818

Demonstrated 2PP-fabricated helical micromachines coated with magnetic nickel, swimming in rotating magnetic fields. This is the primary reference for GT2-fabricated magnetic helical microswimmers using IP-Dip. Includes fabrication parameters, swimming speed characterization, and cargo transport demonstrations.

---

Ghosh, A. and Fischer, P.
Controlled propulsion of artificial magnetic nanostructured propellers.
Nano Letters, 9(6), 2243-2245, 2009.
DOI: 10.1021/nl900186w

Demonstrated the smallest magnetically propelled helical swimmers to date (300 nm diameter, 2 um long) fabricated by glancing angle deposition. Not GT2-fabricated, but establishes the minimum scale for magnetic helical propulsion and the field strength requirements.

## Soft Microrobots

Palagi, S., Mark, A. G., Reigh, S. Y., Melde, K., Qiu, T., Zeng, H., Parmeggiani, C., Martella, D., Sanchez-Castillo, A., Kapernaum, N., Giesselmann, F., Wiersma, D. S., Lauga, E., and Fischer, P.
Structured light enables biomimetic swimming and versatile locomotion of photoresponsive soft microrobots.
Nature Materials, 15, 647-653, 2016.
DOI: 10.1038/nmat4569

Demonstrated soft microrobots that swim by light-driven shape changes, mimicking the ciliary beating of microorganisms. The robot body is a liquid crystal elastomer that deforms when illuminated with structured light patterns. Relevant for GT2 users working on stimuli-responsive soft microrobots.

---

Hu, W., Lum, G. Z., Mastrangeli, M., and Sitti, M.
Small-scale soft-bodied robot with multimodal locomotion.
Nature, 554, 81-85, 2018.
DOI: 10.1038/nature25443

Demonstration of a millimeter-scale soft robot capable of multiple locomotion modes (rolling, swimming, walking, jumping) driven by programmed magnetization patterns. Highly relevant for GT2 users designing multi-modal soft microrobots using IP-PDMS.

---

## Shape-Morphing and Origami Structures

Felton, S., Tolley, M., Demaine, E., Rus, D., and Wood, R.
A method for building self-folding machines.
Science, 345(6197), 644-646, 2014.
DOI: 10.1126/science.1252610

Demonstration of self-folding structures using shape memory composite materials. The folding design principles described here are applicable to GT2-printed bilayer structures and anisotropically swelling IP-PDMS geometries.

---

## Medical Microrobotics

Nelson, B. J., Kaliakatsos, I. K., and Abbott, J. J.
Microrobots for minimally invasive medicine.
Annual Review of Biomedical Engineering, 12, 55-85, 2010.
DOI: 10.1146/annurev-bioeng-010510-103409

Comprehensive review of microrobotics for medical applications, covering drug delivery, surgery, and diagnostics. Provides the application context and performance requirements (size, speed, biocompatibility) that GT2-fabricated medical microrobots must meet. Essential reading for anyone working toward in vivo microrobotics applications.
