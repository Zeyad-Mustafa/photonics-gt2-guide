# Foundational Papers

## Overview

These are the papers that established two-photon polymerization as a fabrication method, characterized its physical and chemical basis, and demonstrated its core capabilities. If you are writing a paper or grant proposal involving GT2 printing, the most-cited papers in this list are the standard references for the technique.

Each entry includes the paper title, authors, journal, year, DOI where available, and a brief description of what the paper established.

---

## The First 2PP Fabrication Demonstrations

Maruo, S., Nakamura, O., and Kawata, S.
Three-dimensional microfabrication with two-photon-absorbed photopolymerization.
Optics Letters, 22(2), 132-134, 1997.
DOI: 10.1364/OL.22.000132

This is the founding paper of two-photon polymerization as a 3D fabrication technique. Maruo and colleagues demonstrated that tightly focused femtosecond laser pulses could polymerize a photocurable resin in a sub-diffraction-limited volume by two-photon absorption, enabling the fabrication of true 3D microstructures that no other technique could produce at the time. Every subsequent paper in the field cites this work. If you cite only one foundational paper, cite this one.

---

Kawata, S., Sun, H. B., Tanaka, T., and Takada, K.
Finer features for functional microdevices.
Nature, 412, 697-698, 2001.
DOI: 10.1038/35089130

This short Nature paper demonstrated a 2PP-fabricated structure (a bull sculpture) with feature sizes below the diffraction limit — approximately 120 nm — by operating near the polymerization threshold. It established that 2PP could break the diffraction limit and produce nanoscale features, which was a significant conceptual advance. Widely cited as the proof-of-concept for sub-diffraction 2PP.

## Physical Basis of Two-Photon Absorption

Goppert-Mayer, M.
Uber Elementarakte mit zwei Quantensprungen (On elementary acts with two quantum jumps).
Annalen der Physik, 401(3), 273-294, 1931.
DOI: 10.1002/andp.19314010303

This is the original theoretical prediction of two-photon absorption by Maria Goppert-Mayer, published in 1931 — decades before lasers existed. She calculated that absorption of two photons simultaneously could drive an electronic transition, and derived the selection rules and cross-sections. The unit of two-photon absorption cross-section (the GM, Goppert-Mayer unit, 10 to the minus 50 cm to the 4th power times s per photon) is named after her. This paper is cited for historical completeness and as the theoretical basis of the entire field.

---

Kaiser, W. and Garrett, C. G. B.
Two-photon excitation in CaF2:Eu2+.
Physical Review Letters, 7(6), 229-231, 1961.
DOI: 10.1103/PhysRevLett.7.229

The first experimental demonstration of two-photon absorption, performed shortly after the invention of the laser. This paper verified Goppert-Mayer's predictions and opened the experimental field of nonlinear optics.

## Photopolymerization Chemistry

Fouassier, J. P. and Lalevee, J.
Photoinitiators for Polymer Synthesis: Scope, Reactivity, and Efficiency.
Wiley-VCH, 2012.
ISBN: 978-3527332533

This is the standard textbook reference for photoinitiator chemistry. It covers the full range of photoinitiator types, their absorption spectra, radical generation mechanisms, and efficiency under single- and multi-photon excitation. Relevant for understanding why specific photoinitiators are used in IP-series resins and how to interpret the effect of laser power on polymerization yield.

## Sub-Diffraction Resolution in 2PP

Fischer, J. and Wegener, M.
Three-dimensional optical laser lithography beyond the diffraction limit.
Laser and Photonics Reviews, 7(1), 22-44, 2013.
DOI: 10.1002/lpor.201100046

A comprehensive review of methods for achieving sub-diffraction feature sizes in two-photon lithography, including STED-inspired approaches (stimulated emission depletion applied to lithography), threshold effects, and inhibition chemistry. Explains both the physical mechanisms and the practical parameter space for sub-100 nm 2PP features. Highly recommended reading for anyone using the 63x objective at its resolution limits.

---

Gan, Z., Cao, Y., Evans, R. A., and Gu, M.
Three-dimensional deep sub-diffraction optical beam lithography with 9 nm feature size.
Nature Communications, 4, 2061, 2013.
DOI: 10.1038/ncomms3061

Demonstrated 9 nm feature sizes in a specially formulated photoresist using a two-beam approach (writing beam plus depletion beam). While this uses specialized equipment beyond the standard GT2 configuration, this paper established the ultimate resolution limits of photopolymerization-based lithography and is a key reference for resolution discussions.

## Voxel Shape and Fabrication Accuracy

Juodkazis, S., Mizeikis, V., Seet, K. K., Miwa, M., and Misawa, H.
Two-photon lithography of nanorods in SU-8 photoresist.
Nanotechnology, 16(6), 846-849, 2005.
DOI: 10.1088/0957-4484/16/6/019

Systematic characterization of voxel shape as a function of laser parameters. Established the ellipsoidal voxel model and the relationship between NA, wavelength, and axial-to-lateral aspect ratio. Foundational for understanding why the axial voxel is always 3 to 7 times larger than the lateral voxel depending on objective NA.

## Shrinkage and Dimensional Accuracy

Sun, H. B., Takada, K., Kim, M. S., Lee, K. S., and Kawata, S.
Scaling laws of voxels in two-photon photopolymerization nanofabrication.
Applied Physics Letters, 83(6), 1104-1106, 2003.
DOI: 10.1063/1.1599951

Quantitative characterization of how voxel dimensions scale with laser power and exposure time. Provides the experimental basis for the power-voxel-size relationships described in the voxel sizing sections of this guide.

---

Stampfl, J., Baudis, S., Heller, C., Liska, R., Neumeister, A., Kling, R., Ostendorf, A., and Spitzbart, M.
Photopolymers with tunable mechanical properties processed by laser-based high-resolution stereolithography.
Journal of Micromechanics and Microengineering, 18(12), 125014, 2008.
DOI: 10.1088/0960-1317/18/12/125014

Systematic study of the relationship between photopolymerization conditions and mechanical properties of the resulting polymer. Covers shrinkage, Young's modulus, and hardness as functions of exposure. Useful background for understanding why UV post-curing improves mechanical properties.
