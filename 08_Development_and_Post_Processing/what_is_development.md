# What Is Development

## The State of the Sample After Printing

When the GT2 finishes a print job, the substrate sits in the sample holder with a drop of resin on its surface. Inside that resin drop, the laser has polymerized a solid structure — but the surrounding liquid resin has not reacted and remains fluid. The solid structure is invisible from above because it is submerged in and covered by liquid that has nearly the same refractive index.

At this point the sample looks identical to how it looked before printing. There is no visible structure, no raised surface, no color change. The only thing that has changed is the molecular structure of the resin at the focal points the laser traced through.

Development is the process that makes the printed structure physically present and accessible by removing everything that was not polymerized.

---

## What Happens Chemically During Development

The development solvent (typically PGMEA — propylene glycol monomethyl ether acetate) is chosen because it dissolves uncured monomer freely but does not significantly dissolve or swell the cured polymer network.

The mechanism works as follows:

Uncured resin consists of mobile monomer molecules that are small enough to diffuse freely through the solvent. When the substrate is submerged in PGMEA, the uncured monomer molecules dissolve into the solvent and are carried away from the substrate surface.

Cured polymer consists of crosslinked chains that are chemically bonded to each other in a network. This network is too large and too tightly bonded to dissolve in PGMEA. It remains in place as the uncured resin around it is removed.

The result: the solid polymerized structure remains on the substrate, and the surrounding liquid resin is washed away into the solvent.

Over time, as development continues, the PGMEA becomes increasingly loaded with dissolved monomer. If development continues for too long after the uncured resin is removed, the solvent may begin to interact with the cured polymer — causing slight swelling, softening, or dimensional changes. This is why development time is a controlled parameter, not a step to leave running indefinitely.

## What the Structure Looks Like After Development

Immediately after removing from the development solvent and before drying, the structure is submerged in IPA. At this point it may be visible as a slightly hazy or translucent region on the substrate if the structure is large enough to see under a stereomicroscope.

After drying, the structure becomes visible under an optical microscope. Small structures (below 10 um in all dimensions) require an SEM to image properly. Larger structures (above 50 um) are often visible to the naked eye as a slight texture or reflection change on the substrate surface.

---

## Why Development Fails

Development fails in one of four ways:

Underdevelopment: development time was too short and uncured resin remains in channels, gaps, or internal volumes of the structure. Fine features appear filled or blurred. Solution: increase development time and ensure fresh solvent.

Overdevelopment: development time was too long and the solvent has begun to affect the cured polymer. Structures appear slightly rounded, dimensions are larger than expected, or very fine features have softened and merged. Solution: reduce development time, use fresh solvent.

Mechanical failure during development: the solvent flow physically dislodges thin features from the substrate, or the drag force during transfer between solvent baths knocks over tall thin structures. Solution: handle gently, minimize agitation, consider critical point drying for fragile structures.

Collapse during drying: as the IPA evaporates from the surface of the structure, surface tension pulls adjacent features toward each other. Thin features that survive development can collapse at this stage. Solution: use critical point drying or supercritical CO2 drying to eliminate the liquid-gas interface during drying.
