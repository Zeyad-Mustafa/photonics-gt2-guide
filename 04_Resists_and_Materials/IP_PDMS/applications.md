# IP-PDMS — Applications

---

## Microfluidic Devices

The most mature application of IP-PDMS is printing **active microfluidic components** — parts that must deform during operation:

- **Membrane valves:** A thin PDMS membrane (5-20 um thick) deflects under pneumatic pressure to open or close a flow channel. Printing these in IP-PDMS eliminates the need for manual PDMS membrane casting and bonding.
- **Peristaltic pumps:** Sequential actuation of multiple PDMS membranes moves fluid through a channel.
- **Passive check valves:** Elastic flap structures that open under forward flow and close under backflow.

These geometries require the elasticity of PDMS — rigid resists (IP-S, IP-Dip2) would crack under the actuation forces.

---

## Soft Robotics

IP-PDMS enables **3D-printed soft microrobots** and compliant mechanisms:

- Microgrippers that close when heated or pneumatically actuated
- Helical propellers for magnetically driven swimming microrobots (when doped with magnetic particles after printing)
- Compliant hinges and flexures for MEMS-scale mechanisms

The key advantage over conventional PDMS molding: IP-PDMS can print **true 3D geometries** (overhangs, internal channels, interlocking structures) that are impossible to release from a mold.

---

## Cell Mechanics Research

PDMS substrates are widely used in cell mechanics research because cells respond to substrate stiffness. IP-PDMS microstructures can be printed with controlled mechanical compliance to:

- Study durotaxis (cell migration toward stiffer regions)
- Measure cell traction forces (cells deform micropillar arrays, and the deformation is measured)
- Create substrates with spatially patterned stiffness

---

## Flexible Photonics

IP-PDMS's transparency (~1.41 refractive index) makes it usable for flexible optical waveguides and light-guiding structures that must bend without cracking.
