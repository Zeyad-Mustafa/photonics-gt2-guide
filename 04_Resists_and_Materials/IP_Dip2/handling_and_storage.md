# IP-Dip2 — Handling and Storage

> Improper storage is the most common cause of resist degradation. A vial of IP-Dip2 that was left on the bench in ambient light for a week is not the same material as a properly refrigerated vial. This file tells you exactly how to store and handle it.

---

## Storage Requirements

| Condition | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Temperature | 2-8 C (standard laboratory refrigerator) |
| Light exposure | Protected from all light — store in original dark vial or wrapped in foil |
| Position | Upright, tightly capped |
| Shelf life (unopened) | ~12 months from manufacture date (check vial label) |
| Shelf life (opened) | Use within 6 months; discard if viscosity increases noticeably or turbidity develops |
| Atmosphere | Ambient air is acceptable; no special inert gas required |

> Never store IP-Dip2 in a freezer. Repeated freeze-thaw cycles can cause phase separation or precipitation of the photoinitiator. Refrigerator temperature (2-8 C) is the correct range.

---

## Before Use: Equilibration

IP-Dip2 stored at refrigerator temperature is slightly more viscous than at room temperature. Before printing:

1. Remove the vial from the refrigerator.
2. Allow it to equilibrate to room temperature for at least **30 minutes** before opening.
3. Do not shake or stir — gentle inversion once or twice is acceptable if settling is observed.
4. Keep the vial covered until the moment of dispensing.

Printing with cold resist causes:
- Higher viscosity leading to bubble formation at the objective-resist interface
- Slightly shifted polymerization threshold (cold resist is marginally less sensitive)

---

## Dispensing

1. Work in subdued light or under yellow/red safe-light conditions if possible. Avoid fluorescent overhead lights directly above the open vial.
2. Use a clean plastic or glass dropper, or a micropipette with a fresh tip.
3. Dispense only what you need. Return the cap immediately after dispensing.
4. Do not return unused resist from the substrate back into the vial — contamination risk.

---

## Personal Protective Equipment

IP-Dip2 contains **acrylate monomers** and **photoinitiators**. These compounds are:

- **Skin sensitizers** — repeated skin contact can cause allergic sensitization. Once sensitized, even trace contact causes a reaction.
- **Eye irritants** — avoid all eye contact.
- **Harmful if ingested** — do not eat, drink, or touch your face while handling.

Required PPE:

| Item | Specification |
|---|---|
| Gloves | Nitrile (not latex — acrylates penetrate latex quickly) |
| Eye protection | Safety glasses or goggles |
| Lab coat | Standard chemical-resistant lab coat |
| Work area | Fume hood or well-ventilated bench |

---

## Spill Cleanup

1. Do not let spilled resist cure under ambient light — clean up immediately.
2. Absorb the liquid with paper towels or absorbent pads (wear gloves).
3. Wipe the area with IPA-dampened cloth.
4. Dispose of all contaminated materials as chemical waste.

Do not rinse spilled resist into the drain.

---

## Waste Disposal

- Liquid IP-Dip2 (uncured) must be disposed of as **photopolymer/acrylate chemical waste**. Collect in a labeled waste container and follow your institution's hazardous waste procedures.
- Cured IP-Dip2 (solid structures, developed samples) can generally be disposed of as solid chemical waste. Confirm with your institution's EHS office.
- Developer waste (PGMEA + dissolved resist): collect as halogen-free organic solvent waste.

---

## Signs of Degraded Resist

Discard and replace IP-Dip2 if you observe any of the following:

- Increased viscosity or gel-like consistency
- Cloudiness or white precipitate in the liquid
- Yellow or brown discoloration (slight straw color is normal; dark yellow is not)
- Smell change (fresh IP-Dip2 has a faint acrylate odor; strong or ammonia-like smell indicates degradation)
- Consistently higher threshold power needed compared to previous calibration
